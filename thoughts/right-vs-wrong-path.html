Right vs Wrong Path

There is no right path to find — only the one you make right.

2026-04-18 · Kasen Teoh

Recently I received an offer from a Series C startup. After many months of interviewing, I was thrilled. I had applied because the work genuinely interested me, especially the focus on detecting AI deepfakes and social engineering. I saw real potential for the company to expand into identity, and not only human identity but agentic identity as well. This is a field I care about and believe will grow, because today the primary interaction is between humans and AI, while in the future a different kind of relationship will form: AI agent to AI agent.

When the offer came, I was prepared to accept it. I was also nervous, because it was a startup. Coming from a larger company, I had grown comfortable with the things bigger companies offer: the work life balance, the pay, the people, the predictability. A startup meant less pay and harder grinding in exchange for more growth and more ownership.

The offer arrived on a Friday, and that evening I went to dinner with a good friend. I will call him X. I told him I was going to accept, that it was an opportunity I had earned and that I should go for it. But X could see I was hesitant, and he called me out. He asked whether I was taking the offer only because I did not want to interview anymore.

That hit me. I had to sit with it. Was I accepting because I truly wanted to, or because I thought it was what I should be doing? In tech, in the Bay Area, joining a startup and taking that risk is something I had quietly come to see as impressive, the kind of move that opens doors later.

Each of those is its own question.

Desire. I do believe this startup has real potential, and it sits in a field I have no direct experience in, which would have made it genuinely fun to learn, especially since I had already been reading about agentic identity. But I had to ask myself honestly: was I passionate enough about this field to commit to it for at least two years? Was I willing to trade my work life balance and my salary for this particular direction?

Path. This carried even more weight. I had always assumed that, being in the Bay Area and in tech, the natural next step after big tech was a startup, taking the risk in pursuit of exponential growth. I had decided, somewhere along the way, that this was the right thing to do.

So I went back and forth, and underneath it all I was afraid of making the wrong choice. By my own standards the startup was the right decision, and I felt I should push myself to take it. But if it was so clearly right, why was it so hard? Was I nervous simply because it was unfamiliar, outside my comfort zone? Or was it genuinely not for me?

Being afraid of the wrong decision left me paralyzed. I started asking friends, mentors, and my parents what they thought. Everything they raised, I had already considered: the risk, the pay cut, the work, the effort.

At some point I realized I was no longer asking for opinions. I was looking for someone to tell me definitively what to do, so that I would have a scapegoat if it went wrong. If the startup failed, I could tell myself I had only done it because someone I trusted said it was the right choice.

Here is the part that took me longest to see. I handle external failure reasonably well. When someone else rejects me, there is no one to put on trial. But a decision I freely make leaves me as both the judge and the accused. So I was reaching for other people's approval to spread the weight of a verdict I was afraid to carry alone.

What made this stranger is that I do not behave this way everywhere. When I analyze markets, or model a financial decision, or design something technical, I trust my own judgment completely, often against the consensus. But those domains have something life decisions do not: a way to be scored. Without an objective right answer, I stop trusting myself and start outsourcing to whatever other people think is right.

The root of it is perfectionism. I try to optimize for the best outcome, to make the best decisions, to never allow myself a mistake. Here I was afraid of two symmetrical failures at once: choosing a startup that might fail, or passing on a startup that might succeed. That fear of being wrong in either direction is what froze me.

What I failed to understand for a long time is that in situations like this there is no right or wrong choice waiting to be discovered. The optimal move is to choose a path you believe in and then make it the right one. That can mean accepting the risk, learning, and building relationships regardless of the outcome. It can mean continuing to grow where you already are. It can mean continuing to interview, now that you understand the level of risk you are actually willing to accept.

The offer itself became the lesson. Everyone wants to be perfect, to be the best. But mistakes are allowed. Mistakes are part of how we become our own version of perfect.

In the end I did not accept the startup offer, and I kept interviewing. The way I finally made peace with it was simple. I wanted to take this risk, and I did not. For a while I believed that not taking it made me a failure. It does not. It only means I was not comfortable with this particular risk. I used that clarity, and the discomfort that came with it, to keep going.
